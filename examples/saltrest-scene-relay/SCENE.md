The gray had come early to Saltrest, as it always did. Mother Vell stood on the dock stones before the first of the fishermen would wake, her shawl pulled tight against the cold that was not quite winter. The harbor lay flat and strange—not calm, but empty. That was the word that kept returning as she watched the pale sky bleed into the darker water: empty. No gulls. No creaking of moorings. No breath of wind that might have carried the salt-stink she'd learned to expect from thirty years of seasons here.

She could feel it in her sternum, that wrongness. The sea was waiting for something.

Vell had been born on the east shore, in a village of limestone and loud water, before her calling had sent her inland to a monastery for five years. When she'd come to Saltrest, she'd thought the fear would fade. It hadn't. Every dawn since had brought that old cold to her chest—the knowledge that water that deep held weight, held secrets, held things that should stay buried. The nets came up heavier each week, though the catch grew only stranger, and she stayed back from the water's edge, as she always did, letting the younger ones haul and sort.

Then—a sound that broke the stillness. The creak of rowlocks. Out on the pale, featureless water, a single boat was emerging from the mist, oars moving in slow, steady strokes toward the shore.

By the time the boat's hull scraped the shale, the fishermen had come. They gathered as they always did, wordless, drawn by the pale pull of the dawning. The nets lay coiled in the boat's belly, heavier than they should have been, darker than fish-catch silver. When the eldest fisherman and his mate hauled the first of them, Vell saw the weight resist, saw the rope dig deep into the shallows. Something in the mesh that was not alive.

They spread the net across the stones. The smell reached her first—salt and time and something underneath, like the sweet rot of meat left in sun. Vell's hand went to her mouth. The bones spilled out then, pale and long, entangled in the cordage like a drowned man's final gesture. A hand. The curve of a rib cage. Teeth in a jaw still strung with dark matter. Human.

The crowd fell silent. Then murmuring began—a sound Vell could feel in her teeth, brittle and rising. Someone was crying. The eldest fisherman backed away from the net, his mouth working soundlessly. But Vell did not move closer. She stood where she stood, twenty paces back from the water's edge, her shawl trembling, her breath coming shallow and fast. The sea had given up its dead. Three weeks of this, she understood now. Three weeks of gifts.

A voice rose above the murmur—one of the fishermen calling out. Vell looked up. Three strangers had stepped onto the dock, their clothes still dry, their eyes on the scattered bones.

They moved with the weight of people who had seen hard things. Not soldiers, she thought—something else. The taller one, who walked first, had the manner of someone accustomed to making decisions. The other two followed in a way that suggested trust, not obedience. Their gear was worn, practical, and they did not flinch at the smell or the sight of the remains.

The villagers drew back by slow increments, as though pulled. Vell felt it too—a shift in the air, the old hope and the old fear running together. These strangers had come for something. Or they had come to take something away. She could not quite read which.

Then the fisherman's daughter spoke, her voice small but clear. "The Mayor. He went out alone to the caves, seven days past. No word since. No boat back."

The words fell like a stone into still water. Mayor Halrik. Of course. The absence she'd been feeling all morning was not only the wrongness of the sea, but the wrongness of his silence—his daily walk along the strand, his presence at the Salted Anchor. Gone. And these strangers coming now, just as the catch had turned to bone. Vell's hand tightened on her shawl. She needed to speak to them. Whatever they were, wherever they had come from, they needed to know what Saltrest had become. She needed to know what they would do about it.

She took a breath and began to move through the crowd toward them.

The taller one turned as she approached, reading something in the set of her jaw or the gray at her temples. Vell spoke plainly, as she had learned to do in the monastery—no coaxing, no delay.

"I will not go to the water," she said. "I cannot speak what the sea knows without knowing it myself. But the Mayor rowed out toward the north shore, where the caves mouth into the dark. Kelpwater Cove. He was certain something was calling, and he went alone as he did everything in these last weeks—as though he had already decided not to come back."

She paused, her fingers working against the rough weave of her shawl. "There is a woman who came through three days past. Coria Sleet. A traveler—dark work about her, the kind of knowing that makes the old wives pull their children inside. She bought rope. Good lantern oil. The kind meant to last long in deep places."

One of the strangers was already looking toward the northern headland, where the caves lay hidden in the deeper gray.

"Go to Kelpwater Cove," Vell said. "Go while the tide is still climbing. And if you hear singing—the kind that seems to come from inside the water itself—do not answer it."

The villagers had fallen quiet again. The scattered bones still lay on the dock stones, and the pale light was starting to break across the harbor's rim. Vell could have sworn the water itself was rising, inch by slow inch, as though the cove was breathing in. The strangers moved toward the north path, and no one moved to stop them.
